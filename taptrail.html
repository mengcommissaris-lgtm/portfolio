M1.2 Design Research

TapTrail

U&S | T&R | C&A | B&E | M,D,C

A design research project exploring how to help employees take regular breaks from inactivity in the workplace. Increasing levels of sedentary behaviour among office workers can negatively affect both physical and mental health. Existing solutions often rely on gamification elements such as leader boards, progress reports, and badges. However, these approaches do not always sustain engagement in the long term. This project investigated how game design principles could be used to encourage and maintain healthy break-taking behaviour among employees.

TapTrail gave me a last opportunity to put everything I learned into practice. The decision to work on this project with my partners has helped me improve the last parts of expertise I wanted to improve, namely, communicating with stakeholders, working together with business partners and create a product with market potential. At the same time, it broadened my horizons through an introduction to game design, adding a new perspective to my understanding of user engagement and behaviour.

The process
At the start of the project I was unsure which design method to use. I wanted an approach that strongly integrated user participation and context for continuous feedback and adjustments throughout the design process. However, I learned from previous projects (M1.1 and M1.2) that I had the tendency to neglect the related research throughout the project. For both projects I had chosen for the Iterative Design Process, as this provides the envisioned user engagement and improvements of concepts. I decided to combine the Iterative Design Process with the Design Thinking Bootleg steps, using its tools and structured phases to guide my work and support evidence-based decision-making.

Looking back, this was a good decision. It encouraged me to reflect at the end and beginning of each cycle on what to do next and how to approach it. As a result, my evaluations became more focused and provided a clear direction for subsequent design decisions.
